Grammar: Using Prepositions

An interactive guide to learning the rules of prepositions for time, place, connections, and academic writing. Explore the rules and test your knowledge.

Prepositions link nouns, pronouns, and phrases to other words. While many are fixed expressions, the rules below help define relationships in time and space. Use the filters to focus on a specific category.

in Time

Months, seasons, years, centuries, historical periods, time of day.

  • in August / in the summer
  • in 1985
  • in the evening
  • in the 19th century
  • in two hours (after a period of time)

at Time

Specific time of day, noon, night, midnight, mealtimes, age.

  • at 2:30
  • at night / at noon
  • at breakfast
  • I learned to use a computer at 12.

on Time

Days of the week, specific dates.

  • on Friday
  • on April 21st

since Time

From a period of time up to the present (when it started).

  • I have been a student since 2004.

for Time

How long a period of time has been.

  • I have been a student here for 2 years.

from...to... Time

The beginning and end of a period of time.

  • My appointment is from 13:30 to 14:00.

until Time

A period of time up to a specific point in time.

  • I cannot go dancing until I finish this chapter.

by Time

In the sense of "at the latest"; due date.

  • You must return your book by April 21st.
  • My essay is due by the end of the week.

in Place

When something is inside a place (enclosed within limits).

  • in class / in Victoria
  • in the book
  • in the car / in a taxi
  • You look serious in this photo.

at Place

Located at a specific place (a point); for events; place for typical activity.

  • at the library
  • at a concert / at a party
  • at the cinema / at school / at work

on Place

Being on a surface; for a certain side; for a floor; for public transport; for TV/radio.

  • I left the keys on the table.
  • ...the third door on your left.
  • My apartment is on the fourth floor.
  • I forgot my phone on the bus.

to / from Place

Moving toward a specific place (goal) / For the origin or starting point.

  • Every morning, I take the bus to campus.
  • I used carrots from my garden.
  • I received a suspicious email from my bank.

towards Place

Movement in direction of something.

  • I suddenly saw a dog running towards me.

across Place

Movement from one side to another.

  • There is a coffee shop across the street.
  • I swam across the lake.

through Place

Movement from one side to another but "in something".

  • I entered the room through an open window.
  • You have to go through the kitchen.

between Place

A place "in the middle" of two or more separate people or things.

  • I was standing between my friend and his parents.

among Place

A place "surrounded" by more than two people or things as a group.

  • France is among the countries of Western Europe.

of Connection

Shows the first belongs to or is part of the second; relation.

  • The keyboard of my computer is pink.
  • Richard is the son of Audrey.
  • He is a good friend of mine.

with Connection

Second is a feature of the first; people/things are together; specific thing used.

  • ...the computer with a pink keyboard.
  • I am studying with Tara.
  • My roommate killed a spider with a book.

by Connection

Describe an action in a more general way.

  • My roommate saved my life by killing the spider.

about Academic

For topics (in the sense of "with regard to/concerned with").

  • ...books about the history of ancient Egypt.

for Academic

With a purpose/giving a reason (shortened "for the purpose of").

  • ...important for understanding how society works.

from Academic

The origin, cause, or agent of something.

  • ...hard to guess from the individual words.

in Academic

Inclusion, location, or position within a time period or limits.

  • You can see this in works by contemporary authors.

on Academic

The basis for something.

  • ...proceed on the assumption that...

of Academic

Belonging to, relating to, or connected with; causation; origin.

  • The results of the investigation...
  • The root of the problem...
  • ...died of poisoning...

by Academic

Describe a direct cause or agent.

  • ...he was killed by strangulation by the Mad King...

with Academic

Connected to something and near something; using something.

  • ...agreement with statements regarding...
  • ...conclude with the available data.

between Academic

Marking a connection.

  • ...the relationship between education, diet and health.

Learning prepositions just takes time and practice. Much of their use is idiomatic, so memorizing fixed phrases is key. Here are some tips to help you master them.

Do not directly translate prepositions from your first language, as one preposition may correspond to several different prepositions in English. A good way to learn which preposition to use is to consult an English learner's dictionary. You can also use Google Books Ngram Viewer to see the frequency of phrases in standard English.

Reading texts in English is helpful to learn prepositional phrases in context. Choose any text that interests you and identify all prepositions, with a specific emphasis on the noun or verb that goes with it. To memorize them, create your own "flashcard" with the prepositional phrase and a drawing that depicts its usage and/or meaning.

Listening attentively to speakers' use of prepositions in conversations, movies, songs, and presentations will help you extend your vocabulary. In turn, practice using prepositions in everyday conversations with classmates and friends. Start by focusing on five prepositional phrases and then progressively integrate new ones. Practice makes better!
